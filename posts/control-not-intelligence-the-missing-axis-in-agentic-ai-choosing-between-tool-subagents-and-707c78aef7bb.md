<h3>Control, Not Intelligence: The Missing Axis in Agentic AI (Choosing between Tool, Subagents and Skills)</h3><blockquote>“The most important question in an agentic system is not how smart a component is — it’s who decides what happens next.”</blockquote><p>For the last two years, <em>agentic AI</em> has become the default language we use to describe almost everything that involves LLMs doing more than answering a single prompt. We call chatbots agents. We call workflows agents. We call autonomous systems agents. The word has become so overloaded that it now explains very little.</p><p>This is the problem that <strong>“Agent, Sub-Agent, or Tool? A Practitioner’s Guide to Orchestration in Agentic AI Systems”</strong> sets out to fix. And it does so with a deceptively simple move: we frame the entire debate away from <em>intelligence</em> and toward <em>control</em>.</p><p><a href="https://www.techrxiv.org/users/1016007/articles/1385959-agent-sub-agent-skill-or-tool-a-practitioner-s-guide-to-extending-agentic-ai-systems">https://www.techrxiv.org/users/1016007/articles/1385959-agent-sub-agent-skill-or-tool-a-practitioner-s-guide-to-extending-agentic-ai-systems</a></p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*I4K8TIvmnjqufjbr25AWNQ.png" /></figure><h3>The Practitioner’s Dilemma</h3><p>Imagine you’re building a system that can research a topic and synthesize findings. On paper, the requirement sounds simple. In practice, it immediately forces a deep architectural choice. Do you implement this capability as a callable function that returns search results and leaves synthesis to a central model? Do you encapsulate it as a reasoning module that can explore, iterate, and self-critique, but only when invoked? Or do you let it exist as a fully autonomous component that decides for itself when research is needed, what to look for, and when it is done?</p><p>Most teams do not explicitly answer this question. They drift into a solution, borrowing terminology from demos, frameworks, or blog posts, and only later discover that something feels off. The system is hard to debug. Failures are difficult to reproduce. Governance reviews raise uncomfortable questions about accountability. The same word — <em>agent</em> — has been used to describe everything from a single function call to a continuously running autonomous process. When vocabulary collapses, architecture quietly collapses with it.</p><blockquote>When architectural language blurs, systems become harder to reason about, harder to test, and harder to trust.</blockquote><p>This paper argues that the confusion persists because we have been optimizing for the wrong mental model. The industry keeps asking how intelligent a component is, when the more consequential question is far simpler: who is actually in control?</p><h3>The Control Axis (Not the Intelligence Axis)</h3><p>The paper’s central move is almost disarmingly simple. Instead of debating intelligence, sophistication, or model size, it proposes a single diagnostic question: <strong>who decides what happens next?</strong> That question cuts through marketing language and lands directly on system behavior. Control, not cognition, determines how a system fails, how it is tested, how it is monitored, and how it is governed.</p><p>Once you start looking through this lens, many popular assumptions fall apart. A GPT-4 powered component that performs deep multi-step reasoning but returns immediately to its caller does not control its own future. No matter how impressive the output looks, it is still subordinate. By contrast, a minimal rule-based process that continuously observes an environment and decides when to act owns its execution loop. Architecturally, that second system is far closer to what classic AI literature has always meant by an agent.</p><p>The paper visualizes this idea as a spectrum rather than a binary. On one end sit components that are fully controlled from the outside. On the other end sit components that are self-directed, persistent, and autonomous. Between them lies an important middle ground that modern systems rely on far more than they admit.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*I4K8TIvmnjqufjbr25AWNQ.png" /></figure><h3>Tools: Capability Without Agency</h3><p>A tool, as defined in the paper, is not characterized by simplicity or lack of machine learning. It is characterized by obedience. A tool is invoked with structured inputs, performs a bounded operation, and returns an output. It does not ask why it was called. It does not decide whether to retry, escalate, or continue. Every decision about its use lives somewhere else.</p><p>This is why tools are so attractive in production systems. Their bounded nature makes them predictable. Their interfaces make them testable. Their behavior can be logged, audited, and reasoned about after the fact. A vision model that labels objects in an image, a search API that returns ranked results, an embedding generator that maps text to vectors — all of these may involve complex models internally, but architecturally they behave like functions. They execute and then relinquish control.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*ukfQci1QR0gnX2EhsG0tMg.png" /></figure><p>The paper’s analogy is precise and memorable: tools are like muscles. They can be extraordinarily powerful, finely tuned, and specialized, but they never decide when to flex. That decision belongs to something else. This philosophy echoes older systems traditions, from Unix pipelines to service-oriented architectures, where composability and restraint are features rather than limitations.</p><h3>Sub-Agents: Delegated Cognition, Bounded Autonomy</h3><p>Sub-agents occupy the most interesting and most misunderstood position in modern agentic systems. A sub-agent is allowed to reason, to explore alternatives, to plan, to call tools, and even to critique its own intermediate results. What it is not allowed to do is persist on its own terms. It does not own its lifecycle. It is created by a parent, constrained by explicit budgets, and terminated when its task is complete.</p><p>This distinction is subtle but critical. Sub-agents represent delegated cognition rather than autonomy. They exist precisely because some tasks require judgment and iteration, yet still demand centralized control. A research sub-agent can search, filter, and synthesize sources, but it cannot decide that research should continue indefinitely. A policy analysis sub-agent can weigh regulations and edge cases, but it cannot act beyond the scope it was given.</p><p>Thinking of sub-agents as consultants is helpful. They bring expertise and independent reasoning to a well-scoped problem, but they operate under a contract. They deliver a result and then step aside. This pattern allows systems to scale reasoning capacity without fragmenting control, which is why the paper argues that hierarchical orchestration with sub-agents is the natural resting place for most serious production systems.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*KBGGiJiyf3WZ_lAH-cYPbw.png" /></figure><h3><strong>Skills: Procedure Without Autonomy</strong></h3><p>Between tools and sub-agents lies a construct that deserves far more attention: skills. They are easy to overlook because they do not look dramatic. They do not own goals. They do not control execution. They do not decide what happens next. Yet architecturally, they solve a very real problem. A tool gives you capability. A skill gives you know-how.</p><p>A search API can retrieve documents. A calculator can compute metrics. A code executor can run scripts. But none of these encode the procedure for how they should be used in a particular domain. Skills do. They package domain-specific workflows, checklists, and step-by-step guidance into a form the system can load and follow when appropriate. A legal review process, a data analysis playbook, a slide creation standard, an internal compliance checklist — these can live as explicit procedural modules rather than being buried inside prompts or assumed by the model.</p><p>The deeper innovation is not autonomy. It is progressive disclosure. Instead of loading every instruction and every tool definition into context at all times, skills can remain lightweight until needed. At startup, the system may only load a short description of each skill. When a request matches the scope of a skill, the full procedural guidance is brought into context. Until then, it stays dormant. This dramatically reduces context overhead while preserving reasoning capacity for the task itself.</p><p>More importantly, skills cleanly separate knowledge from control. Organizational expertise becomes version-controlled, auditable, and portable. It lives outside the model weights and outside fragile prompt engineering. The orchestrator still decides when to invoke a skill. The skill does not decide for itself whether it should act. This is why skills belong on the control spectrum. They increase competence without increasing autonomy. They extend what a system can do without fragmenting who decides what happens next.</p><p>In a landscape rushing toward ever more autonomous agents, skills offer a quieter alternative. Not every upgrade requires more independence. Sometimes what systems need is not more freedom, but better instruction.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*KxJIzi1fJrAcGN-cPxBmLA.png" /></figure><h3>Agents: Owning the Loop</h3><p>An agent, in the strict sense used by the paper, is defined by control closure. It owns a goal, maintains state over time, observes its environment, and decides when to act. Crucially, it does not need to be re-invoked. Once started, it runs until it succeeds, is stopped, or exhausts its resources.</p><p>This is the point at which many systems cross an invisible threshold. The moment a component controls its own observe–think–act loop, it becomes qualitatively different from everything that came before. Debugging shifts from inspecting inputs and outputs to reconstructing trajectories. Testing shifts from unit tests to simulations. Governance shifts from permissioning functions to supervising behavior.</p><p>The paper is deliberately unsentimental about this transition. Autonomy is not presented as an upgrade. It is presented as a trade-off. Autonomous agents introduce non-determinism, operational overhead, and real governance risk. They are powerful, but expensive, and the paper’s position is clear: unless the domain genuinely demands autonomy, it is usually the wrong default.</p><figure><img alt="" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1024/1*RfEDtba95OarSxYEj-F7wQ.png" /></figure><h3>Orchestration Patterns: Architecture Shapes Behavior</h3><p>Once tools, sub-agents, and agents are clearly distinguished by control, orchestration patterns stop feeling arbitrary. They emerge naturally from where control is allowed to flow. Systems with a single decision-maker invoking tools form tool-centric designs. Systems that centralize control but delegate reasoning form hierarchical designs. Only systems where no single entity owns execution qualify as truly multi-agent.</p><p>One of the paper’s most useful heuristics is also one of its simplest: if the central coordinator disappears and the system collapses, you never had a decentralized multi-agent system. You had hierarchy. This test alone cuts through a remarkable amount of confusion in how modern frameworks are described.</p><p>The broader lesson is that architecture shapes behavior far more than model choice. Emergence, coordination, and robustness are properties of control flow, not parameter counts.</p><h3>Why This Paper Matters</h3><p>What makes this paper stand out is not that it invents new components, but that it restores precision to familiar ones. By grounding definitions in control rather than hype, it gives practitioners a vocabulary that maps cleanly to real systems. The result is not theoretical purity for its own sake, but practical guidance that shows up immediately in better debugging strategies, clearer ownership boundaries, and more maintainable architectures.</p><p>The conclusion is almost conservative, and that is precisely why it feels timely. In a moment when autonomy is often treated as an aesthetic choice, the paper insists on discipline. Start with tools. Escalate to sub-agents when reasoning demands it. Move to full agents only when the domain truly earns that complexity.</p><blockquote><strong><em>Autonomy should be earned through necessity, not assumed by default.</em></strong></blockquote><h3>Final Thought</h3><p>As agentic systems move from demos to infrastructure, the cost of architectural ambiguity grows quickly. Systems that blur control boundaries become difficult to test, difficult to govern, and difficult to trust. Systems that are explicit about who decides what happens next scale more cleanly, both technically and organizationally.</p><p>Understanding whether you are building a tool, a sub-agent, or an agent is not a semantic exercise. It is the difference between an intentional system and an accidental distributed one — and between elegance and entropy as these systems grow.</p><p><em>Paper: “</em><a href="https://www.techrxiv.org/users/1016007/articles/1385959-agent-sub-agent-skill-or-tool-a-practitioner-s-guide-to-extending-agentic-ai-systems"><em>Agent, Sub-Agent, or Tool?
